# The Story Behind Centinela

## What we set out to do

We wanted to help people see danger coming. Natural hazards like floods, heavy rain, unstable ground, and earthquakes are measured constantly by serious science, but almost none of that reaches the person who actually lives in harm's way. The goal was to take those real measurements and turn them into one clear answer for a place, in plain words, so that a resident could open a map, understand their situation in seconds, and know what to do, without needing a meteorology degree or a second language.

Two things mattered from the start. The information had to be honest: a useful estimate, clearly labeled as our own model, that always points people back to their local emergency authority rather than pretending to be it. And it had to stay alive on its own, because a safety tool that quietly goes stale is worse than no tool at all.

## The hard parts

The first weeks were humbling. Before any of the helpful features could exist, the system had to be able to read its own data, and for a while it simply could not. The live feeds were not connected, early attempts came back empty, and there was a long stretch of false starts just to get fresh measurements flowing and to confirm they were actually fresh and not days old.

Then came the human problems, which were harder than the technical ones. The risk score we were proud of meant nothing to ordinary people. Notifications arrived twice, or not at all. The map showed the wrong things. Each of these was a reminder that being correct is not the same as being useful.

## How the project changed

It started small and narrow: one kind of hazard, one region. It grew into something much closer to its purpose. It now watches many cities at once and blends several hazards into a single risk level, while still keeping an eye on places it cannot fully monitor yet and saying so plainly.

The biggest shift was away from the number and toward the person. We stopped showing a percentage and started saying what is happening and what to do, written in the resident's own language and read aloud so it works for someone who cannot or will not read a screen. We made it something you can keep on your phone like any other app, one that still opens and shows the last thing you saw even when the signal drops. And we opened an honest channel for emergency services to consume the same alerts directly.

Behind all of this, the quiet guardian grew up too. What began as a manual freshness check became an agent that watches the flow of data on its own, repairs it when a source goes stale, and openly raises a hand when it cannot, instead of hiding the gap.

## What we learned

The number is not the message. People do not act on a statistic; they act on a clear sentence about their safety, in words they already use. Plain language did more for this project than any improvement to the model.

Honesty is a feature, not a weakness. Saying out loud that this is a demonstration, marking anything we simulate, and deferring to the real authorities made people more willing to trust it, not less.

An alert only counts if it arrives, can be heard, and is understood. Translation, a spoken voice, and a simple notification turned out to be as important as the science underneath them.

Reliability has to be invisible. The data must refresh itself, and the system has to admit when something is broken, because the moment people sense it might be wrong is the moment it stops being useful.

## Where it landed

Today the tool watches hazards across many cities and turns live measurements into one plain risk level for each place: low, a warning, danger, or critical. It shows that on a map alongside recent history, explains what it means and what to do in the resident's language, reads it aloud, and sends a notice the moment risk rises for a place someone cares about. And it keeps itself fed and honest, with a guardian working in the background so the people who depend on it do not have to think about whether it is still running.

It is a demonstration, not a replacement for official warnings. But it points at the thing worth building: real science, made understandable and reachable, for the people who need it most.
